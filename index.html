Section 01

What You're Building & Why

One product, six fabrication methods, three design applications — built by educators, for educators.

🏭

What Is a Factory Experience?

Inspired by the Autodesk University Factory Experience — an annual event where professionals walk a mini factory floor to build a consumer product from concept to completion — this session adapts that model for K–12 educator professional development.

Instead of injection molding and CNC at industrial scale, we use the tools actually found in school makerspaces: 3D printers, laser cutters, vinyl cutters, metal fabrication machines and industry-standard design software.

📻

Why a Bluetooth Speaker?

The retro AM radio aesthetic isn't just charming — it creates a product complex enough to require six distinct fabrication processes and three different design applications, yet simple enough to assemble in a PD session.

Every component teaches a different skill set. Together, they demonstrate how real products require the convergence of multiple disciplines — exactly the kind of integrated thinking STEM education should foster.

🎓

Who Is This For?

This experience is designed for educators attending the 2026 Pine Crest Innovation Institute — teachers, instructional coaches and administrators interested in bringing maker-centered learning into their schools.

The goal is not just to build a speaker. It is to experience the full design-fabrication-assembly pipeline so you can envision how to teach it.

3D Printing the Frame

The enclosure prints in PLA on a Prusa MK4s. Key decisions: layer height (0.2mm for speed vs. 0.15mm for finish), infill percentage (15–20% balances strength and material), support structures for overhangs and print orientation to minimize visible layer lines on the front face.

Section 03

Software & Design

Two applications handle every design task in this build — but they serve fundamentally different purposes.

3D Parametric CAD

Autodesk Fusion

Fusion handles the speaker's 3D-printed enclosure. This is parametric modeling — dimensions are driven by constraints and relationships, so changes propagate automatically. Students learning Fusion gain exposure to the same workflow used in product design and mechanical engineering.

Key skills introduced: Sketch constraints, extrude/revolve, fillet/chamfer, component assembly, STL export, design for additive manufacturing.

Fusion Screenshot
Parametric model of the speaker enclosure — show timeline, sketch geometry or section analysis.
📌

Free for educators via Autodesk Education plan. Runs on Mac/Windows. Chromebook access via browser is limited.

2D Vector Design

Adobe Illustrator

Illustrator drives four separate outputs: the laser-cut wood faceplate, the laser-engraved acrylic dial, the Cricut-cut vinyl sticker, and the metal-cut "Pine Crest" lettering. Each requires different file-prep considerations — stroke colors for cut vs. engrave, SVG export for Cricut and DXF for the xTool.

Key skills introduced: Vector vs. raster, pen tool, stroke/fill for machine output, artboard management, SVG/DXF/PDF export, kerf compensation.

For Educators: Design Software in the Classroom

🔄

Fusion vs. Tinkercad

Many schools start with Tinkercad. Fusion is the logical next step — but the learning curve is real. Consider Tinkercad for grades 6–8, Fusion for grades 9–12. The parametric workflow in Fusion teaches design thinking that Tinkercad's block-based approach doesn't.

📂

One File, Many Machines

A powerful takeaway: a single Illustrator file can drive a laser cutter, vinyl cutter and metal cutter. The skill isn't learning four machines — it's learning to prepare one design for multiple outputs. That's transferable.

🔑

Licensing & Access

Both Fusion and Illustrator are available free or at steep discounts for educational institutions. Fusion is completely free for educators. Adobe offers institutional licensing. Document your access plan before committing to a curriculum.

Section 05

Safety Considerations

Every machine in this build carries specific risks. Here's what educators need to know before running any of these processes.

⚠️ General Principle

No one should operate any equipment without proper training specific to that machine. Manufacturer training materials, institutional safety protocols and local regulations always take precedence over the general guidance on this page. This is an overview, not a substitute for formal training.

🖨️

3D Printing (Prusa MK4s)

Primary risks: Hot nozzle (200 °C+), heated bed (60 °C+), minor pinch points on moving axes.

Material safety: PLA produces low-level particulate emissions. Ventilation recommended, especially for prolonged printing. Not acutely toxic, but err toward caution.

PPE: None for normal operation. Heat-resistant gloves if handling bed immediately after printing.

⚡

Laser Cutting (Epilog Fusion Pro)

Primary risks: Class 4 laser — direct or reflected beam causes permanent eye damage and burns. Fire risk from combustible materials.

Ventilation: Mandatory fume extraction. Never cut PVC, polycarbonate or materials containing chlorine.

PPE: Laser safety glasses rated for the machine's wavelength. Never operate with the lid open or interlocks bypassed.

✂️

Vinyl Cutting (Cricut Maker 3)

Primary risks: Minimal. The blade is small but sharp — use the blade housing tool to change blades, not fingers.

Material safety: Adhesive vinyl is non-toxic. Transfer tape and weeding tools are low-risk. This is the safest station in the build.

🔩

Metal Cutting (xTool Metal Fab)

Primary risks: Sharp metal edges on cut parts. Potential for metal particulates. Noise.

Material safety: Stainless steel is inert, but cut edges can be razor-sharp. Deburring is required after cutting.

PPE: Cut-resistant gloves for raw output. Safety glasses. Follow manufacturer's fume extraction guidance.

Safety in the Classroom Context

📋

Before You Bring It to School

Check your school's and district's policies on makerspace equipment. Many districts require formal risk assessments, parent permission forms and documented training logs. Some insurance carriers have specific exclusions. Do the paperwork first.

👥

Supervision Ratios

Laser cutters and metal fabrication should never be operated by students without direct adult supervision — typically 1:1 at the machine. 3D printers and Cricut machines can operate with broader ratios once students are trained.

🚨

Emergency Preparedness

Fire extinguisher rated for materials in use (Class B for plastics). First aid kit with burn supplies. Emergency stop procedures posted at each machine. Know your school's emergency protocols.

Section 06

For Educators

How to think about bringing integrated fabrication experiences into your own classroom or makerspace.

🧩

This Is Not a Lesson Plan — It's a Design Pattern

The Factory Experience demonstrates a structure for project-based STEM learning: start with a finished product, decompose it into components that each require different skills and tools, then guide learners through the full pipeline from design to assembly.

You don't need this exact product. You don't need these exact machines. The transferable idea is: convergence of multiple disciplines into a single tangible outcome.

📐

Scaffolding for Students

Middle school: Simplify to 2–3 fabrication methods. Use Tinkercad instead of Fusion. Pre-cut complex components; let students assemble and customize.

High school intro: Students design one component (e.g., the faceplate) and receive the rest pre-made. Gradually expand ownership.

Advanced HS / post-secondary: Students own the full pipeline — design all components, troubleshoot fabrication, iterate on fit.

⏱️

Time Estimates

As a PD session: 2–3 hours with pre-fabricated components + guided assembly.

As a classroom unit: 3–5 weeks depending on student ownership. Fusion modeling alone can take 5–8 class periods for beginners.

Print time per frame: 4–8 hours depending on settings. Plan for overnight prints or batch scheduling.

Scaling Strategies

One printer, 30 students: Stagger prints over weeks. Assign teams to different stages simultaneously.

No metal cutter: Substitute laser-engraved or 3D-printed lettering. Design file prep stays the same.

No laser cutter: CNC-routed wood or hand-cut alternatives. Trade-off is precision and time.

✅

Assessment Ideas

Process portfolios documenting each design iteration and fabrication step.

Design review presentations where students justify material choices, dimensions, tolerances.

Functional testing: Does it pair? Does the sound quality meet a rubric? Does it assemble without forcing?

Section 07

Lessons & Takeaways

What the Factory Experience is meant to leave you with — beyond the speaker itself.

The Big Idea

No single tool, skill or discipline produces a finished product. Real manufacturing — like real learning — requires the integration of multiple knowledge domains, iterative problem-solving and collaboration across specialties. The Factory Experience compresses that reality into something you can hold in your hands.

🔀

Integration Over Isolation

Schools tend to teach tools in isolation: a 3D printing unit here, a laser cutting project there. This build demonstrates what happens when you require them to work together. Tolerances between the 3D-printed frame and laser-cut faceplate force designers to think across processes. That's the real lesson.

🔧

Failure Is Part of Fabrication

Prototyping this speaker involved failed prints, misaligned cuts, kerf errors and parts that didn't fit. The finished product you see is the result of iteration. Show your students the failures. They're more instructive than the final version.

🚪

Access Is Not Binary

You don't need a $50,000 laser cutter to teach the principles behind this build. You need the design thinking. A student who can prepare an Illustrator file for laser cutting has a transferable skill — even if your school sends the file to a service bureau.

🎯

Start With the Product

Don't start with "let's learn Fusion" or "let's learn the laser cutter." Start with "let's build this thing" — then let the tools serve the outcome. Students engage differently when the learning has a tangible destination.

What to Bring Back to Your School

1. Audit your tools. You don't need all four machines. What do you have? What can those machines make together?

2. Pick a product. Choose something your students will want to keep. A phone stand, a desk organizer, a lamp, a clock. Reverse-engineer it into components that use your available tools.

3. Design the convergence. The magic isn't the individual tools — it's the moment when parts made by different processes come together and either fit or don't. Design your project to require that moment.

4. Document everything. Photograph prototypes, failures and final products. Build your own version of this website. Share it with colleagues. That's how maker culture scales.

5. Connect with your community. The Innovation Institute is a starting point. Reach out to other maker educators, join Autodesk's education community, attend regional fab lab meetups. You don't have to figure this out alone.
